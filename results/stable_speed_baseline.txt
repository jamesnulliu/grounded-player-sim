STABLE PER-INDIVIDUAL-SPEED BASELINE (paper-readiness audit P1 item): does
the evolving latent add think-time value beyond a *stable* per-individual
calibration -- not just beyond a fully memoryless twin? A reviewer versed in
response-time psychometrics will want a van der Linden-style baseline (a
per-person latent speed + item/position difficulty, no within-session
dynamics) as the thing the evolving latent must beat, not just a
zero-history control.

We already have this control implemented and trained jointly on the timing
head -- `StaticIndividualInjector` (`run_ec(control="static")`, arm "B2"):
a per-player learned constant (no accumulation over the trajectory) fed into
the same lognormal timing head as D. This is the neural-net analogue of a
van der Linden lognormal RT model (per-person speed theta_p + item/position
difficulty from the shared history features, fit by gradient descent instead
of closed-form MLE). It was already reported for E-C1 on the MOVE channel
(results_ec.md: "dynamic > static-individual embedding, pooled -0.120") but
never scored on TIMING -- this closes that gap.

Data: cached g4_data/ec{2017,2019}/dataset.jsonl.gz (100 players each, same
cohorts as the concentration/G4 experiments). session split, epochs=15.
2026-07-13.

     already captures most of what the evolving latent captures for THIS
     cohort's move choice.

VERDICT: cohort-dependent, same qualitative pattern already disclosed for
other controls on this pair of cohorts (see "E-C timing + cohort dependence"
in project memory / results_ec.md's G4 co-fit section, where 2019-07 is
consistently the weaker/noisier cohort -- its concentration decision-sd is
also ~2x 2017-04's, see results/concentration_variance_controlled.txt).
Report honestly as a cohort-dependent result, not a universal win: the
evolving latent clears the "stable per-individual speed" bar on 2017-04 but
not 2019-07. Do not claim this baseline is beaten unconditionally; scope the
claim to "beats a stable-speed baseline on at least one real cohort,
cohort-dependent like the other secondary controls," and keep the primary
headline (D vs memoryless B) as the one that is robust across both cohorts.
