Abstract

Chess engines have evolved from search-based systems optimized solely for strength to neural policies capable of modeling human decisions across much of the rating spectrum.
Maia-3, the strongest human-like move policy for chess, models the typical moves
of a given rating but does not model high Elo strength well (2500+ Lichess Elo) nor
has an extensible architecture for modeling individual play-style. On the other hand, search-based engines like Stockfish are
far stronger than any known human but struggle in modeling human-like play.

1 Introduction

Computer chess has moved through three eras. Search-first engines – Deep
Blue’s massively parallel alpha–beta (Campbell et al. , 2002 ) and its
descendants – reached superhuman strength by looking further ahead than
any person. Neural engines then replaced hand-tuned evaluation with learned
ones: AlphaZero’s self-play policy and value networks
(Silver et al. , 2017 ) , NNUE evaluation inside Stockfish
(The Stockfish developers, 2026 ) , and lately pure transformer engines that reach
superhuman strength at a fraction of AlphaZero’s compute
(Monroe and Team, 2024 ) . A third line inverted the goal: instead of
the best move, predict the human move. Maia
(McIlroy-Young et al. , 2020 ) trained rating-banded networks; Maia-2
(Tang et al. , 2024 ) unified skill levels; Maia-3 (Monroe et al. , 2026 ) 
is the current most accurate human-move predictor. Around it, a scattering
of work has touched the individual: per-player fine-tuning
(McIlroy-Young et al. , 2022 ) , behavioral stylometry
(McIlroy-Young et al. , 2021 ) , and prototype-initialized per-player
embeddings (Tang et al. , 2026 ) .

These threads have not been put together. The engine era knows what is
objectively strong; the human-modeling era knows what is typical at a
rating; neither, alone, predicts what a specific strong human will
do. The gap is concrete: Maia-3’s rating conditioning is weakly grounded
above 2600 and it is blitz-trained, so it is weakest exactly
where prediction is hardest – and
it models the typical player of a rating, not the idiosyncrasies that make
an individual recognizable. The difficulty for the individual half is
confounding: stronger players simply make better moves, so a naive
per-player embedding recovers mostly rating.

Matilda puts the pieces together with a residual
formulation. A frozen, rating-conditioned Maia-3 explains what a typical
player of that strength would do; a small set-transformer re-ranker then
learns only the deviations – engine-informed corrections where the
human prior runs out, and per-player style as a compact vector that
modulates the re-ranking. Engine knowledge enters as per-candidate
features from an offline search; identity enters as 32 floats. Because
rating-typical play is already absorbed by the frozen base, the style
vector has gradient signal only for style, making Elo-disentanglement an
architectural property that we also measure directly.

The closest prior attempts at modeling the individual either fine-tune
Maia per player with plain NLL (McIlroy-Young et al. , 2022 ) –
effective, but requiring thousands of games and a full model per player,
and weakest exactly where prediction is hardest – or initialize per-player
embeddings from prototype players at club level (Tang et al. , 2026 ) .
Matilda changes the architecture instead: a scalable
set-transformer whose two extension points – engine-feature candidate
tokens and a player-style context input – unlock high-Elo human-move
prediction and economical personalization from one shared model.

3 Method

Figure 1: Matilda overview. A frozen Maia-3 reads the position, the
last eight positions, and both players’ ratings, producing its move
likelihoods, an internal position reading, and a board-attention map. These,
together with the time control (an input to the re-ranker only, not to
Maia-3), become one context token (optionally personalized by a 32-d
player-style vector, dashed red), and Maia-3’s sixteen most likely moves
become candidate tokens carrying prior statistics, optional offline
engine scores (dashed green), and a learned move fingerprint. A two-layer
set-transformer attends over all seventeen tokens and emits one adjustment
per candidate, which is added onto Maia-3’s original move likelihoods before
a softmax over all legal moves; the adjustment head starts at zero,
so the untrained system is exactly Maia-3. Corner badges give tensor sizes;
dashed elements are optional.

3.1 A Set Transformer with Swappable Engine Input

The architectural bet is that improving a strong human prior is a
re-ranking problem: given the moves the prior already considers
plausible, decide which of them this player, in this situation, actually
plays. Two requirements follow. Candidates must be scored jointly –
choosing among moves is a comparison, not independent scoring – and the
model must accept whatever evidence about the candidates happens to be
available, from any source, without retraining.

Matilda therefore treats candidates as a set of tokens.

centipawn, centipawn-loss-vs-best, engine rank, and an engine-top-choice
flag; and (iii) a learned

3.2 Personalization: Player Embedding Design

Identity enters as one 
32 32

id and passed through a zero-initialized linear projection before
being added to the context token (next subsection). Zero initialization
makes personalization a strict extension: with the projection untrained –
or a player unknown – the personalized model is exactly the base. Row 
0 

is reserved as an explicit generic player: during training the true
id is replaced by 
0

0.1 0.1

generic row is itself trained and unknown players fall back to it

entirely frozen (
0.2 0.2 
M trainable parameters), so their gradient carries
only what the rating-conditioned base cannot already explain –
systematic deviations from rating-typical play. This is the residual
formulation’s disentanglement argument: strength is absorbed by the frozen
base, and the embedding is left to encode style; we verify the claim
directly with rating-recovery probes. New players never seen in training
receive embeddings post-hoc: the base and the shared projection stay
frozen and only the new rows receive gradients, so the style space learned
from strong players transfers unchanged – the mechanism behind the
down-the-ladder and few-games experiments.

5 Discussion and Conclusion

Matilda supports a simple thesis: a scalable set-transformer
re-ranker over a frozen human prior improves human-move prediction along
two orthogonal dimensions – engine features, which add value
specifically where rating-conditioned human priors fail (the top of the
rating range), and compact per-player embeddings, which model
individual players economically and are Elo-disentangled by construction:
style in the true sense, not strength. The division of labor is clean. The
predictive work is done by the engine dimension: sub-

family, surviving memorization and sibling audits, and costing nothing

constrained. The new-player curve makes the economics concrete: an
embedding pays for itself after roughly two games.

Future work.

Because Matilda has no positional
encoding and carries move identity per token, it scores any
candidate set, of any size, in any order: which moves to search, and how
deeply, becomes an action space for a reinforcement-learned – and
ultimately player-personalized – search controller, which we develop in
companion work. More broadly, the candidate-token contract invites an
ecosystem: any developer’s search – a conventional engine, a budgeted
shallow search, or a novel long-range planning strategy – can feed
Matilda ’s candidate set without retraining the re-ranker. A second
direction is explanation: the architecture’s
candidate tokens and attention structure are a natural interface for
generative models that explain predicted moves in natural language,
an area where human-move prediction is still behind.
