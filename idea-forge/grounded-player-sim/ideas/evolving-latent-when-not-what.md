# When, Not What: an evolving per-individual latent state predicts a person's future think-time, not their choices

<!-- ============ I. Body (generator writes in full at generation time) ============ -->

## 1. One-liner + plain version

One-liner (technical): A per-individual latent state that evolves over a person's own action-and-timing trajectory — isolated from mere history-conditioning by an equal-capacity, same-input *memoryless twin* evaluated on strict future splits of that person's data — is far more legible in *when* a person acts (think-time) than in *what* they choose (move / answer), and this asymmetry holds across six years of real Lichess chess and against released think-time models, while the same latent generalizes to student-response prediction and population recovery on eight real knowledge-tracing datasets.

Plain version: Systems that imitate specific people usually freeze each person as a fixed thing — a rating, a fixed embedding, a persona paragraph. But real people drift: a chess player who just lost plays the next game differently; a student who struggled through ten problems answers the eleventh differently. This project carries a small "state" that updates as a person acts, and then does the honest test almost nobody does: it builds a twin model that is identical in every way (same size, same inputs, same optimizer) except that it is forbidden to remember anything across steps, and asks whether the remembering actually buys predictive power on that person's *future* games. The finding is a clean asymmetry. The accumulated state helps a lot at predicting *how long* the person will think before acting, and almost not at all at predicting *which* move they pick. Concretely, on real Lichess chess the evolving state beats its memoryless twin at future think-time in all eight era-by-backbone conditions from 2017 to 2023, and even improves a released think-time model (Allie); but a probe from the state to move choice recovers essentially nothing (R^2 = 0.009).

## 2. Which gap it fills

Negative space (extracted from the manuscript's own related-work synthesis, `documents/related_work.md` and `documents/paper.md` §1–2): history-conditioned behavioral models confound two capabilities — *accumulating* a persistent state over a person's trajectory versus merely *seeing* recent context — and no prior line separates the two with a **capacity-matched, same-input control on real human behavior with a timing target**. The individual axes are each occupied: LATTE (arXiv:2605.26612) runs an evolving-vs-static matched comparison but as a soft token into a *frozen* LLM, no timing target, no per-decision oracle domain; Ailed (arXiv:2603.05352) asserts evolving chess emotion but has no human-subject validation (dynamics asserted, not measured); ChessMimic (arXiv:2606.04473), Maia-2 (arXiv:2409.20553), Allie (arXiv:2410.03893) and Elo-Disentangled embeddings (arXiv:2606.25176) all *freeze* the person (static per player or cohort-level on the clock) and none validate on a strict per-individual *future* split; response-time psychometrics (van der Linden 2006/2007) uses a *stable* per-person speed, not an evolving within-session state. The empty space is the intersection: an equal-capacity evolving-vs-memoryless twin, same inputs, strict future split, timing target, per-decision oracle domain — the control that alone can attribute a win to accumulated state.

Why it is a real problem and not a fake need: simulated humans are becoming infrastructure (tutoring systems tuned against simulated students, game platforms training human-like opponents, agents stress-tested against user models; `paper.md` §1). All of these need models of *specific* people, and all current approaches freeze the person, so they cannot track visible within-session drift (tilt, fatigue, clock panic). The paper does not merely assert the drift matters — it produces the surprising, non-obvious finding that the drift is legible in *timing* but essentially absent from *choice*, reframing "model the person" as "model when they act, and carry a state that accumulates."

## 3. Technical-route sketch (executed project — what WAS done)

Input: per-person trajectories of decision points (chess games with per-move clocks; knowledge-tracing response logs). Output: held-out future next-action and next-think-time likelihoods, plus a battery of isolating controls.

1. **Decision-point interface + history features.** [off-the-shelf] Reduce every domain to a sequence of decision points, each bundling game/item state, legal action set, a per-decision external-oracle reference signal, a time signal, and the recent outcome stream; compute a small vector of interpretable history features `h_t` (time pressure, post-loss recency, fatigue, momentum) that *both* model arms receive. One interface runs unchanged on chess and knowledge tracing.
2. **Evolving latent vs equal-capacity memoryless twin.** [GRU recurrence off-the-shelf; **the isolating control is where the novelty lands — has-precedent for evolving-vs-static (LATTE), no-precedent for the equal-capacity same-input twin with the recurrent hidden state zeroed at every step, on a per-decision oracle domain with a timing target**] A gated recurrent unit accumulates `z_t = f(z_{t-1}, h_t)`; the twin shares architecture, parameter count, inputs, and optimizer, differing only in that the incoming hidden state is zeroed every step (accumulation removed, nothing else). A width sweep verifies the win is not a capacity artifact.
3. **Swappable backbones + latent-only timing head.** [backbones off-the-shelf: board-native MLP/conv over FEN planes, KT logistic head, Qwen3 0.6B–8B via sglang; has-precedent zero-inflated log-normal] The think-time head is a (zero-inflated, to handle 1-second clock quantization and ~9% zero premoves) log-normal whose parameters read *only* the latent, never the board encoder — making the timing comparison structurally backbone-independent.
4. **Training and strict future-split evaluation.** [off-the-shelf SFT; has-precedent leakage-safe causal future-sessions split] Fit by supervised learning on move NLL + λ·timing NLL over each person's *earlier* trajectory; score the held-out *later* sessions. Causal recurrence + loss masking make warming the latent over the whole trajectory leakage-safe. Significance by bootstrap over *players* (the independent unit), 95% CI, plus P(D−B<0).
5. **Released-model baselines + mechanism battery.** [released models off-the-shelf; the add-on-over-released-model framing and the synthetic-known-state probe/clamp are has-precedent to no-precedent] Escalate the timing baseline to Maia-2 position entropy and to Allie's actual released think-time head, testing the latent as an add-on `(base+z)−base` and as evolving-vs-static over locked Allie; add synthetic players with a *known* recorded hidden state (probe R^2 + causal clamp), time-pressure/rating concentration analysis, KT population recovery (Wasserstein / precision-recall), and a hidden-vs-verbal injection-channel comparison.

Resources (as used): Lichess open database (six clocked/unclocked cohorts, ~70k decisions each); eight real KT datasets (ASSISTments 09/12/15/17, KDD Algebra + Bridge, Spanish, Statics) + EdNet-KT1; OGS Go (honest negative); 2×A100 for the tier-1 5-seed×2-backbone sweep and the LLM SFT arm; the core evolving-vs-memoryless comparison runs deterministically on CPU.

Most-likely-to-break step (as the manuscript itself flags): step 3's core D-vs-B numbers use *small from-scratch* move backbones, so the move-channel ceiling under a strong (engine-informed) move model is open. Fallback actually taken: the timing head reads only the latent (backbone-independent by construction) and step 5 shows the latent adds value *over released models*, retiring the weak-backbone objection for the timing pillar; the move-channel ceiling is left as stated future work.

## 4. How to measure / how to compute

Core estimator: the held-out negative-log-likelihood difference **D−B** (evolving minus memoryless twin), in nats, lower NLL better, so D−B < 0 favors the evolving latent. Three channel likelihoods feed it: think-time NLL from the latent-only (zero-inflated) log-normal head; move NLL from factored from/to logits masked to legal moves; response NLL from the logistic correct/incorrect head. Significance: pool each player's per-seed D−B, bootstrap over players (decisions within a player are correlated and never resampled directly), call a result significant when the 95% CI excludes zero; report P(D−B<0) as a directional-consistency summary.

Escalating-baseline estimators: `(base+z)−base` — the change in held-out think-time NLL from appending the per-step latent `z` to a baseline (Elo+clock, +branching factor, +Maia-2 legal-move entropy, and Allie's released think-time head), with per-player Spearman rank-correlation reported to certify baseline strength (reaching 0.62–0.65 for Allie); and `evolving−static` over a locked Allie prediction plus a learned per-player constant, to separate dynamics from stable per-player calibration.

Mechanism estimators: linear probe held-out R^2 from the trained latent to the *known* synthetic hidden state (0.93 evolving vs 0.65 twin vs 0.009 for the real move channel against deviation-from-Maia-2); causal clamp along the probed state direction, reading the monotone change in predicted move entropy and think-time; concentration = bucket held-out timing D−B by time-pressure tercile, aggregate to one point per player per bucket, normalize by the bucket's own decision-level standard deviation (variance-controlled ratio 2.7–3.6×). Population recovery: Wasserstein-1 and Jensen–Shannon distance of the predicted vs observed per-student accuracy distribution, plus improved precision/recall (Kynkäänniemi et al. 2019) and corr(pred, obs).

## 5. Most fragile assumption + strongest counterexample

Most fragile assumption: that the chess **timing** edge reflects genuine within-session *state dynamics* (the person carrying and evolving a real behavioral state) rather than mere per-person online *calibration* (individualization) — i.e. the model simply learning "this player is generally fast/slow/erratic" from accumulated evidence, which is order-invariant and not the advertised evolving-state story.

The manuscript attacks this itself and the attack partly lands. On knowledge tracing, a temporal-shuffle control shows the response-channel edge is *undiminished* under permutation (−0.0151 shuffled vs −0.0095 unshuffled), so it is explicitly identified as order-invariant individualization, not dynamics; the same shuffle on synthetic timing is also undiminished. Dynamics and individualization are only *fully* separable on synthetic data (where the recorded hidden state permits the probe R^2 = 0.93 and the causal clamp). For real chess timing, the dynamics reading rests on the *concentration signature* (the edge concentrates 2.7–3.6× under time pressure after variance control, and ≈3× for the weakest players — a uniform individualization edge would not concentrate) plus the synthetic probe/clamp — but a faithful real-chess shuffle that would cleanly isolate cross-game dynamics is deliberately **deferred** (a game-level shuffle leaves the within-game clock signal intact; a within-game shuffle violates clock monotonicity), so this specific control is missing on real chess.

Strongest counterexample, already in the data: the when-not-what asymmetry **does not transfer to real education response times**. On ASSISTments 2009 the timing channel is inconsistent across seeds (+0.0003, +0.0045 significant-wrong-sign, −0.0062) and on EdNet-KT1 it is null (−0.0004, CI [−0.0059, +0.0059]), while the response channel stays significant throughout. So the timing claim is scoped to real chess + synthetic KT, on the *unproven* boundary hypothesis that the timing channel requires time to be a strategically managed resource (a chess clock) rather than an incidental UI timer. A hostile reviewer's strongest move: argue the real-chess timing edge is *also* individualization plus a heteroskedasticity artifact of the high-pressure regime; the variance-normalized concentration and synthetic clamp are the defense, but the absence of a decisive real-chess dynamics-isolating shuffle and the small absolute effect sizes (−0.01 to −0.07 nats) leave this contestable. Secondary fragilities the manuscript concedes: small from-scratch move backbones (move-channel ceiling open) and no live human study.

## 6. 1-week minimal validation experiment (executed — reproduces the headline)

Data: one clocked Lichess cohort, e.g. 2017-04 blitz, 100–120 players with ≥30 games and ≥3 sessions, ingested with per-move `[%clk]` (~70k decisions). Implementation: the GRU injector as the evolving arm (persist=True = D) versus the identical injector with the recurrent hidden state zeroed each step (persist=False = B), equal parameters/inputs/optimizer, board-native backbone, a zero-inflated log-normal timing head reading only the latent, trained by SFT on move + λ·timing NLL over each player's earlier trajectory, scored on the held-out later-sessions split; deterministic on CPU, ≥3 seeds, bootstrap over players. Measurement: held-out think-time NLL difference D−B with a 95% player-bootstrap CI.

Support criterion (observed): the CI excludes zero on the negative side and replicates across seeds and backbones — the executed result is D−B ≈ −0.026 [−0.030, −0.022] under the proper zero-inflated head on 2017-04, and negative with every CI excluding zero in all eight era-by-backbone conditions 2017→2023. Falsification criterion: the timing CI includes zero or favors the twin; or the move channel shows a same-magnitude edge as timing (which would dissolve the when-not-what asymmetry the whole contribution rests on). Pending items: none for this minimal experiment (executed); the open extensions are a strong/engine-informed move backbone and a decisive real-chess dynamics-isolating control.

#### Steelman

Systems that imitate specific people freeze them (a rating, a fixed embedding, a persona), yet people visibly drift within a session (tilt, fatigue, clock panic). This project builds the honest control almost nobody runs: an evolving per-individual GRU latent versus an *identical* twin (same params, inputs, optimizer) whose only difference is the hidden state is zeroed each step, scored on strict per-individual *future* splits — so a win is attributable to *accumulation*, not to seeing recent context or to capacity. The clean, surprising result is an asymmetry: the accumulated state buys real predictive power for *when* a person acts (future think-time D−B ≈ −0.026 [−0.030,−0.022], negative with CI excluding zero in all eight era×backbone conditions 2017→2023, and it even improves Allie's released think-time head), but essentially none for *what* they choose (probe R²=0.009). The timing edge concentrates 2.7–3.6× under time pressure after variance control (a uniform individualization edge would not concentrate), and on synthetic players with a *recorded* hidden state the evolving arm recovers it (probe R²=0.93 vs 0.65 twin) with a causal clamp. The methodology (twin control + player-bootstrap + variance-normalized concentration + honest cross-domain negatives) is rigorous cognitive-modeling science, and the reframing "model *when* they act, carrying an accumulating state" is a directly reusable lens for human-like game bots and tutoring simulators.

#### Most fragile assumption / strongest counterexample

The idea's §5 names dynamics-vs-individualization as its most fragile assumption — that is honest and correctly identified, and the concession that the decisive real-chess shuffle is deferred is candid. **But it mis-prioritizes: the more lethal counterexample is the move-channel ceiling threatening the *asymmetry itself*, not just the framing.** If the timing edge is "only" individualization, the when-not-what asymmetry still stands (accumulated per-person evidence helps timing, not choice — a clean finding either way). By contrast, if a strong released move backbone (Maia-3/Chessformer) makes the state *choice*-legible, the asymmetry — the entire "when, not what" contribution — dissolves. The idea's defense ("timing head is backbone-independent, latent adds value over released models") saves only the *timing* pillar; the *choice* pillar (R²=0.009) rests on the small backbones the idea itself flags. So the single most lethal, still-open experiment is: **run the state→choice probe against a strong released move reference; if choice becomes legible, the headline collapses.**
